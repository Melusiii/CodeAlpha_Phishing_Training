Why This Matters

Phishing is responsible for over 90% of successful cyberattacks. A single click on a malicious link can compromise your personal information, financial accounts, or your entire organization's network.

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of cyberattack where criminals attempt to trick you into revealing sensitive information or installing malware by pretending to be a trustworthy entity.

Common Phishing Goals:

  • Stealing login credentials (email, banking, social media)
  • Installing malware on your device
  • Tricking you into sending money or sensitive data
  • Gaining access to corporate networks

Phishing Statistics

  • 1 in 99 emails is a phishing attack
  • 30% of phishing messages get opened by targeted users
  • The average cost of a phishing attack for a mid-sized company is $1.6 million

Recognizing Phishing Emails

Phishing emails often appear legitimate at first glance. Here's how to spot them:

Common Red Flags:

  • Urgent or threatening language: "Your account will be closed!"
  • Generic greetings: "Dear Customer" instead of your name
  • Suspicious sender addresses: "support@amaz0n.com"
  • Poor spelling and grammar: Professional organizations proofread
  • Unexpected attachments: Especially .exe, .zip, or .js files
  • Requests for sensitive information: Legitimate companies won't ask for passwords via email
From: "Amazon Support" <support@amaz0n-security.com>
To: You
Subject: Urgent: Your Amazon account has been locked!

Dear Valued Customer,

We have detected unusual activity on your Amazon account. To prevent unauthorized access, we have temporarily locked your account.

Click here to verify your identity and unlock your account: http://amaz0n-verify.com/account

If you do not take action within 24 hours, your account will be permanently closed.

Sincerely,
Amazon Security Team

What's Wrong With This Email?

  • Generic greeting ("Dear Customer")
  • Misspelled domain (amaz0n instead of amazon)
  • Creates unnecessary urgency
  • Links to a suspicious website
  • Threatens account closure

Phishing Websites

Phishing websites are fake sites designed to steal your information. They often look identical to legitimate sites.

How to Spot Fake Websites:

  • Check the URL carefully: Look for misspellings (faceb00k.com) or wrong domains (paypal.security.com)
  • Look for HTTPS: Legitimate sites use HTTPS (but some phishing sites do too)
  • Check for poor design: Low-quality images, broken links, or formatting issues
  • Verify security certificates: Click the padlock icon in your browser to view certificate details

Hover Before You Click!

Always hover over links to see the actual destination URL before clicking. The displayed text can be completely different from the real link.

Social Engineering Tactics

Social engineering is the psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or divulging confidential information.

Common Social Engineering Techniques:

  • Pretexting: Creating a fake scenario to gain your trust
  • Baiting: Offering something enticing to get you to take action
  • Quid pro quo: Offering a service or benefit in exchange for information
  • Tailgating: Following someone into a restricted area
  • Vishing: Phishing via phone calls
  • Smishing: Phishing via SMS/text messages

Example Vishing Call:

"Hello, this is John from Microsoft Support. We've detected a virus on your computer. Please go to this website and install the remote support software so we can fix it for you."

Remember:

  • Legitimate companies won't call you out of the blue about tech problems
  • Never give remote access to your computer to unsolicited callers
  • Don't provide personal information to callers you didn't initiate contact with

Protecting Yourself from Phishing

Best Practices:

  • Use multi-factor authentication (MFA): Even if your password is stolen, MFA can prevent access
  • Keep software updated: Regular updates patch security vulnerabilities
  • Use a password manager: Helps create and store strong, unique passwords
  • Verify requests: Contact the company directly using official contact information
  • Report suspicious messages: Follow your organization's reporting procedures
  • Educate yourself: Stay informed about current phishing trends

What to Do If You Clicked

  1. Disconnect from the network (turn off Wi-Fi/unplug Ethernet)
  2. Run a full antivirus scan
  3. Change all affected passwords
  4. Enable MFA where available
  5. Report the incident to your IT department
  6. Monitor accounts for suspicious activity

Key Takeaways

  • Always verify the sender of emails and websites
  • Never provide sensitive information in response to unsolicited requests
  • When in doubt, contact the organization through official channels
  • Report all suspicious messages to your IT/security team
  • Stay vigilant - phishing tactics constantly evolve
